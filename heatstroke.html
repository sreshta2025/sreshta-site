Heatstroke

Recognizing and treating dangerous heat-related emergencies

🚨 Medical Emergency

Heatstroke is a life-threatening condition. Call 911 immediately if you suspect heatstroke

What is Heatstroke?

Heatstroke is a severe heat-related illness that occurs when the body's temperature regulation system fails. The body temperature rises to dangerous levels (104°F/40°C or higher) and the person stops sweating, making it impossible to cool down naturally.

Heatstroke is a medical emergency that can cause permanent damage to the brain, heart, kidneys, and muscles if not treated immediately. It can be fatal if left untreated.

Life-Threatening Condition

Heatstroke can kill or cause permanent disability. Time is critical - every minute counts in getting treatment.

Heat Exhaustion vs. Heatstroke

Understanding the difference between heat exhaustion and heatstroke is crucial for proper treatment:

Symptom Heat Exhaustion Heatstroke
Body Temperature Normal to slightly elevated Very high (104°F/40°C or higher)
Sweating Heavy sweating Little to no sweating
Skin Cool, moist, pale Hot, dry, red
Mental State Alert but may feel weak Confused, altered consciousness
Pulse Fast and weak Fast and strong
Emergency Level Serious but treatable Life-threatening emergency

When to Suspect Heatstroke

Heatstroke typically occurs in these situations:

High-Risk Conditions

  • High temperature (over 90°F/32°C) with high humidity
  • Prolonged sun exposure or physical activity in heat
  • Being trapped in a hot car or building
  • Wearing heavy clothing in hot weather
  • Dehydration combined with heat exposure

High-Risk Individuals

  • Infants and children under 4
  • Adults over 65
  • People with chronic medical conditions
  • Those taking certain medications
  • Athletes or outdoor workers
  • People with substance abuse problems

Warning Signs of Heatstroke

  • High body temperature (104°F/40°C or higher)
  • Altered mental state - confusion, agitation, slurred speech
  • Hot, dry skin - red and lacking sweat
  • Rapid, strong pulse
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Headache
  • Dizziness or fainting
  • Seizures (in severe cases)

Step-by-Step Treatment

1

Call 911 Immediately

Heatstroke is a medical emergency. Call for emergency medical help right away.

2

Move to Cool Area

Get the person to a cool, shaded, or air-conditioned area immediately. Remove from heat source.

3

Remove Excess Clothing

Take off unnecessary clothing to help heat escape from the body.

4

Cool the Body Rapidly

Use whatever cooling methods available: cold water, ice packs to neck/armpits/groin, wet towels, or fans.

5

Monitor Vital Signs

Check breathing, pulse, and consciousness. Be prepared to perform CPR if needed.

6

Position Properly

If conscious, have them lie down with legs elevated. If unconscious, place in recovery position.

7

Continue Cooling

Keep cooling until body temperature drops below 102°F/39°C or until emergency services arrive.

Cooling Methods

Most Effective Methods

  • Cold water immersion - If available, immerse in cold water bath
  • Ice packs - Apply to neck, armpits, and groin (major pulse points)
  • Wet towels - Apply cool, wet towels to skin and replace frequently
  • Fans - Use fans to increase evaporation while applying water

Additional Cooling Techniques

  • Spray with cool water from garden hose or spray bottle
  • Use frozen vegetables as ice packs if no ice available
  • Remove to air-conditioned vehicle or building
  • Apply cold, wet sheets to the body

Cooling Caution

Avoid giving fluids if the person is unconscious, vomiting, or has altered mental status. This can cause choking.

What NOT to Do

  • Don't give fluids if person is unconscious or vomiting
  • Don't give alcohol - it increases dehydration
  • Don't give caffeine - it can worsen dehydration
  • Don't use ice directly on skin - wrap in cloth first
  • Don't cool too rapidly - can cause shivering and increase heat production
  • Don't leave person alone - condition can worsen rapidly

Common Mistake

Never assume the person is "just tired" or "sleeping it off." Heatstroke can cause unconsciousness and requires immediate medical attention.

Prevention Tips

  • Stay hydrated - drink water regularly, even if not thirsty
  • Wear light-colored, loose-fitting clothing
  • Avoid outdoor activities during peak heat hours (10am-4pm)
  • Take frequent breaks in shade or air conditioning
  • Never leave anyone in a parked car
  • Acclimatize gradually to hot weather
  • Know your medications - some increase heat sensitivity
  • Check on elderly neighbors and relatives during heat waves

After Treatment

Even after cooling treatment:

  • Hospitalization is usually required for monitoring and additional treatment
  • Complications can develop hours or days later
  • Follow-up care is essential - heatstroke can cause lasting damage
  • Increased sensitivity to heat may persist for weeks

Recovery

With prompt treatment, most people recover from heatstroke. However, the faster treatment begins, the better the outcome.

Important Notes

Risk Factors

Certain factors increase heatstroke risk:

  • Heart disease, diabetes, kidney disease
  • Medications (diuretics, antihistamines, beta-blockers)
  • Alcohol or drug use
  • Previous heat-related illness
  • Obesity
  • Sudden exposure to hot weather

Recognition is Key

The most important skill is recognizing heatstroke early. The combination of high body temperature, altered mental state, and hot, dry skin should trigger immediate emergency response.

Time-Sensitive Emergency

Heatstroke becomes more dangerous the longer it goes untreated. Every minute of delay can mean the difference between recovery and permanent damage.
