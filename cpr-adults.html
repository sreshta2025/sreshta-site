CPR for Adults

Learn cardiopulmonary resuscitation - the essential life-saving technique

🚨 Emergency First

Always call 911 (or your local emergency number) immediately before starting CPR

What is CPR?

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) is a life-saving technique that combines chest compressions and rescue breaths to maintain blood circulation and oxygen supply when someone's heart stops beating or they stop breathing normally.

CPR can double or triple the chance of survival after cardiac arrest. It's designed to manually pump blood through the body until emergency medical help arrives.

Did You Know?

Immediate CPR can increase survival rates from cardiac arrest by 2-3 times. Every minute counts!

When to Use CPR

Use CPR when an adult is:

  • Unconscious - Not responding to verbal or physical stimulation
  • Not breathing normally - No breathing, gasping, or agonal breathing
  • No pulse - No detectable pulse (check for no more than 10 seconds)

Signs That Indicate CPR is Needed:

  • Person is unresponsive to shouting or gentle shaking
  • No normal breathing (chest not rising and falling)
  • Blue lips or face (cyanosis)
  • No pulse at wrist or neck

Important Note

If you're unsure whether someone needs CPR, it's better to start CPR. The risk of injury from unnecessary CPR is much lower than the risk of death from not performing CPR when needed.

Step-by-Step CPR Guide

1

Check Responsiveness

Tap the person's shoulders firmly and shout "Are you okay?" Look for any response.

2

Call for Help

Call 911 immediately. If others are present, send someone to call and ask them to find an AED (Automated External Defibrillator).

3

Position the Person

Place the person on their back on a firm, flat surface. Tilt their head back slightly by lifting their chin.

4

Hand Placement

Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest (lower half of breastbone). Place your other hand on top, interlocking your fingers.

5

Body Position

Position yourself directly over the person's chest. Keep your arms straight and shoulders directly over your hands.

6

Chest Compressions

Push hard and fast at least 2 inches deep. Allow complete chest recoil between compressions. Push at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute.

7

Rescue Breaths (if trained)

After 30 compressions, tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, and give 2 breaths (1 second each). Watch for chest rise.

8

Continue Cycles

Continue 30 compressions followed by 2 breaths. Don't stop until emergency services arrive or the person starts breathing normally.

Hands-Only CPR

If you're not trained in rescue breathing, continuous chest compressions (hands-only CPR) is still very effective and recommended over doing nothing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Insufficient compression depth - Push at least 2 inches deep
  • Wrong hand placement - Use center of chest, not too high or too low
  • Leaning on chest - Allow complete recoil between compressions
  • Interrupting compressions - Minimize interruptions, keep going
  • Wrong compression rate - Maintain 100-120 compressions per minute
  • Giving up too soon - Continue until help arrives

Critical Errors

Never perform CPR on someone who is conscious and breathing normally. Always check for responsiveness first.

Important Notes

Remember the Rhythm

The song "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees has the perfect rhythm for CPR compressions (100-120 beats per minute). Hum it while performing compressions to maintain the correct pace.

Physical Demands

CPR is physically demanding. If others are present, switch every 2 minutes to prevent fatigue and maintain effective compressions.

Legal Protection

Good Samaritan laws protect people who provide emergency medical assistance in good faith. Don't hesitate to help because of legal concerns.

After CPR

Even if the person recovers, they need immediate medical attention. CPR is a bridge to advanced medical care, not a cure.

Training Recommended

While this guide provides essential information, hands-on CPR training from certified instructors is highly recommended for proper technique and confidence.
